Campus-scale read · 2:40 PM
Two sites flagged for intervention

Built surfaces are averaging +9.0°C over the vegetated baseline. Lot C and the Ring Road stop are the priority heat sinks; the M4 conversion shows what a siting decision can claw back.

▲ Strong heat sink Lot C parking

The hottest surface on the network at +14.9°C over control, and it holds heat past 10 PM. Largest contiguous impervious area on this side of campus. Candidate interventions: canopy tree rows between bays, permeable paving at the walking edges. Caution on high-albedo "cool" coating alone — reflected load can raise surface temps on the facades across the drive.

▲ Heat sink flagged Ring Road / Columbia bus stop

Asphalt has held a +11°C surface differential every afternoon this week and keeps radiating 3 hours after sunset — and this is where pedestrians stand and wait with zero shade. Candidate intervention: shade structure or tree planting on the south edge. Highest exposure-hours per square metre of any flagged site.

◆ Watch SLC / QNC courtyard

Concrete plaza runs +7°C at peak with no midday shade — and it's the campus's most-used outdoor sitting area, exactly where people want to be. If any site justifies shade sails or added canopy on comfort-hours gained per dollar, it's this one. Track comfort-hours before and after any change.

▼ Before / after validated M4 site conversion

The M4 build replaced pavement and a demolished pedestrian bridge with a structure plus planted edges. Same point, compared against the pre-construction baseline:

Before (paved, Jul 2024)
ΔT +9.8°C
After (built + planted)
ΔT +3.1°C

A 6.7°C reduction in peak differential — measured evidence that the siting decision reduced, rather than compounded, the campus heat-island effect. This is the before/after loop satellite LST can't close.

● Performing as designed Ken's Garden bed

The planted bed delivers steady cooling — 6–8°C below adjacent pavement all afternoon. One caution: reflected heat off neighbouring concrete and glass pushes the bed's east edge ~2°C warmer, and this week's humidity is in the fungal-risk range for the roses. Green infrastructure works here, but only monitoring keeps it working.

Building-scale read · 2:40 PM
South & west exposures are driving today's loads

RCH's south facade is storing heat it will re-radiate into the evening, while M4's passive design is holding its differential to +3.1°C. Envelope and orientation choices are visible in the surface data.

▲ Envelope liability RCH south facade

Brick at +8.6°C over control, absorbing through the afternoon and re-radiating into the walkway until ~9 PM. RCH is one of the oldest, least-conditioned buildings on campus — microclimate like this can move indoor temps of unconditioned buildings by up to 5°C. Design responses: exterior shading devices on the south glazing, envelope retrofit, or planted buffer to cut reflected gain.

▼ Passive design working M4 solar chimney

M4's solar chimney — one of only a handful in Canadian buildings — uses exactly the solar exposure this network measures (peak 33k lux on site today) to drive natural ventilation instead of mechanical cooling. Post-build surface differential is holding at +3.1°C. This is the per-facade evidence base you'd want when justifying a passive feature on the next project.

◆ Orientation case study QNC courtyard exposure

The courtyard's 48k lux solar load with no shading is what an unprotected south-west exposure looks like in July. For any building fronting this plaza: size overhangs and fins for this measured sun path rather than rule-of-thumb values, and check how new massing shades (or fails to shade) the outdoor space — sky-view factor cuts both ways.

● Green baseline What "good" measures like

Ken's Garden is the reference: canopy + planted ground holds surface temp within ~3°C of ambient all day. Any envelope or landscape decision can be scored against this line — the differential you leave behind is the mechanical cooling load someone pays for, for the life of the building.
