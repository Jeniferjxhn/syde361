Today's critique from the W06 worksheet

First 5 minutes: all group members explain the design, context, and objectives (arch-eng + urban planning vs. plant ops), then open the floor for clarifying questions.

What we're asking for feedback on

  • How to measure success at the decision layer. Success is currently defined at the hardware layer (do sensors read correctly?) but the objective lives at the decision layer — does a planner or arch-eng student make a different, better-informed choice because of the dashboard?
  • Is one paired site (RCH + control) defensible? Or do our claims need to be scoped more narrowly?
  • Temporal validity. Built in July with simulated multi-year data — do reviewers trust one summer month extrapolated across seasons? Material/shading decisions also depend on winter behavior (ice, snow load, freeze-thaw). Caveat the seasonal scope explicitly.

The four core critique considerations

1 · What is the objective?
Convert local environmental data (ambient temp, humidity, surface temp, solar exposure) into actionable insight for arch-eng and urban planning students — find campus heat sinks vs. spots where shade/greenery already work, at a glance.
2 · Which elements relate to it?
Web dashboard (trends, two-persona views), weatherproof box design, and the sensor stack — MLX90614 surface temp, AHT20 ambient temp/humidity, BH1750 solar exposure — feeding the surface-vs-ambient differential.
3 · Are they effective?
Hardware captures the right raw signals; the box protects reading accuracy; impact lives in the software layer.
4 · Why / why not?
Success = the dashboard clearly shows a meaningful difference (or lack of one) between built surface and control, understandable without interpreting raw numbers. Not "did we collect data" but "does it show whether this site is a heat sink."

High-level objectives of the element

  • Move a user from "I intuitively know it's hot here" to "I have fundable, quantified evidence for an intervention."
  • Support real design decisions — Isabella (urban planning interview) said heat-sink data would change where she plans comfort spaces and how much outdoor space she designs.
  • Fit existing workflows — GIS is her "one stop shop"; keep the dashboard structured enough to plausibly become a GIS layer later.

Low-level objectives

  • Let users compare readings against their own intuitive estimate — sharpen the mental model, don't just restate it.
  • Reliable capture of surface temp, ambient temp, and solar exposure at built site and vegetated control, across day/night cycles.
  • Surface a clear surface-vs-ambient differential as the core output (big gap = heat sink, small gap = mitigation working) rather than raw values.
  • Track over time, not snapshots — enables before/after validation of interventions (e.g., did added vegetation actually cool the space?).
During/after the session: the worksheet's final section — "Potential Ways to Improve the Design Towards the Objectives" — must be filled in live. Capture all ideas, not just ones you agree with; let emotions digest before adopting/rejecting. Also pre-empt the known pushback: planners already intuit where hot spots are (parking lots, south/west facades, unshaded plazas) — our differentiators are before/after intervention validation and long-term green-infrastructure monitoring, which satellite LST and intuition can't provide.

Risks, contingencies & open questions

Contingency plan

  • Node count slips below 5: deploy 3 at highest-priority spots (E7, DC, PAC). Don't rush assembly at the cost of stability.
  • WiFi hub too complex: fall back to manual SD-card collection, pushed to the dashboard daily. Loses real-time, keeps everything else.
  • Critique redirects the design: Jul 7–8 are full-team design-response days before any feature code.
  • Hardware reserve: Jenifer or Syra can re-solder / reconfigure / replace a failing node.

Open questions to validate

  • Open  Do planners actually need this, or do they already intuit hot spots? (Differentiate via before/after intervention validation + green-infrastructure monitoring.)
  • Open  Is one paired site (RCH + control) enough to draw conclusions, or should claims be scoped narrower?
  • Risk  July-only data + simulated multi-year set may imply more seasonal generality than it has — caveat explicitly.
  • Risk  Long-term ownership: "who's going to keep it running?" — design for maintainability or it's e-waste in two years.

Design insights condensed from Part 2

  • Protect scarce student focus — frame outputs as go/no-go recommendations, not weather readouts. (I1)
  • Location specificity is everything — students experience the Ring Road crossing and E7 silent study, not "campus temperature." (I2)
  • The tool should feel invisible — more alert system than app. (I3)
  • Indoor–outdoor gap is the core value — the weather app says 28°C; E7 is 16°C and PAC is 32°C. Unserved by every existing tool. (I4)
  • A number is not a decision — a recommendation is. (I5)
  • Mobile students gain most and are the most underserved. (I6)
  • Prevent wasted trips — the recurring failure pattern across interviews. (I7)
  • Trends matter as much as now — the time-series log is a forecast engine if displayed well. (I8)
  • Same reading, different meanings per stakeholder — 20°C wet June day: "manageable" to a commuter, "fungal disease weather" to the rose. (I9)
  • Maintainability decides long-term value — simple wiring, easy batteries, documented setup. (I10)
  • Ecological stewardship is a designed-in feature — same data, reframed for nearby plantings, at zero extra hardware cost. (I11)
